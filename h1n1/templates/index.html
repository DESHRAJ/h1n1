Respiratory viral infections are a common cause for Emergency Department (ED) visits and hospital admissions especially during winter months, adding extra burden to the already busy pediatric clinics and hospitals. Accurate distinction between viral and bacterial infections and additional identification of the causative viral agent is critical for clinical decision making, patient management and outcomes. Early establishment of etiology in primary care or in ED setting will reduce ED and hospital stay and allay patient-parental anxiety. Precisely identifying the causative agent requires specific and sensitive laboratory testing and confirmation. To achieve this, the Children’s Healthcare of Atlanta (CHOA) laboratory, in 2012, began offering a multiplex Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR) test (FilmArray) with a potential turnaround time of 1.5 hours from time of receipt of the nasal swab specimen from ED patients. The PCR test offers accurate detection of viruses RSV, influenza A and B, rhinovirus, parainfluenza virus I – IV, human metapneumovirus, adenovirus and bacterial agents M. pneumoniae, B. pertussis, and C. pneumophila.

The viral panel alone was implemented by the CHOA lab. The bacterial pathogen panel is awaiting FDA approval. The 1.5 hour quick turnaround time (TAT) is expected to have a big impact in reducing the time of patients stay in ED and hospital and antibiotic prescriptions. But, currently there is no widespread means of establishing a rapid and accurate viral causative agent etiology in peripheral healthcare centers. Further, there is no system in place to disseminate the knowledge of the rapidly identified specific viral agents from CHOA laboratories to peripheral centers for detecting additional patients or take preventative measures in the patient’s community. Thus the quick TAT in establishing the specific infective viral agents could well be exploited to improve public awareness and implement public health interventions. In this project, we propose developing and implementing a web-based mashup program integrating specific viral data, date and time of diagnosis with Google map, based on patients zip code. This will provide almost near live dissemination of geospatial information about prevalent viral infections in geographical areas.
